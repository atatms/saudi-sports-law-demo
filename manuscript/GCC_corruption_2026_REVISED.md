# Legal-institutional constraints and corruption control in GCC public administration: a comparative panel study, 2010–2024

**Article classification:** Research paper

---

## Structured Abstract

**Purpose** — This article asks whether two legal-institutional features of the state—rigorous and impartial public administration, and judicial constraints on executive power—are associated with stronger control of corruption in the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) over 2010–2024, and, where they are not, why.

**Design/methodology/approach** — The study builds a balanced country-year panel for Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates (90 observations). The dependent variable is control of corruption from the Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI). The two legal-institutional regressors are drawn from V-Dem; regulatory quality and political stability serve as controls. The analysis runs descriptive and temporal comparisons, correlation and variance-inflation diagnostics, pooled OLS, country, year and two-way fixed-effects models, a random-effects model selected by Hausman test, and an interaction specification, with Wooldridge and Driscoll–Kraay diagnostics and three robustness checks.

**Findings** — The hypothesised direct effects of administrative impartiality and judicial constraints do not hold. Their coefficients are mixed in sign and sensitive to specification. The one stable correlate of corruption control, across every model and both estimators, is regulatory quality. The article reads this not as a failed test but as a finding: in the GCC, corruption-control perceptions track the state's regulatory capacity rather than narrower expert-coded measures of impartiality or judicial constraint, for reasons traceable to the slow temporality of institutional change, limited within-country variation, and the gap between expert-coded and perception-based indicators.

**Originality** — The article is the first to operationalise administrative impartiality and judicial constraint as measurable legal variables in a comparative GCC panel. It reframes corruption control as an institutional outcome shaped by regulatory capacity and the reviewability of executive action, and shows what aggregate governance indicators can and cannot reveal about legal institutions.

**Keywords:** Corruption control; GCC countries; administrative impartiality; judicial constraints; regulatory quality; public administration; panel data.

---

## 1. Introduction

Corruption control is usually explained by political will, administrative capacity, or economic development. Those accounts are well grounded, and they have organised three decades of comparative scholarship (Mauro, 1995; Treisman, 2000, 2007; Svensson, 2005). What they leave underspecified is the legal condition of public authority itself—the terms on which discretion is exercised, reviewed and disciplined. Corruption in public administration is not only an ethical lapse or a failure of efficiency. It is an institutional outcome, more likely where discretion is broad but weakly structured, where executive authority operates without effective constraint, and where regulatory decisions rest on standards that are neither stable, impartial, nor reviewable (Rothstein and Teorell, 2008; Persson et al., 2013; Fukuyama, 2013).

The GCC is a useful place to examine this. The six states share resource-based political economies, large public sectors, and sustained pressure to modernise their regulatory institutions (Ross, 2001; Smith, 2004; Arezki and Brueckner, 2011; Belloumi and Alshehry, 2021). They also differ—sometimes sharply—in the configuration and practical operation of their legal and administrative systems. Regional similarity alongside institutional variation makes the GCC both a coherent unit for comparison and a differentiated one. The question is direct: to what extent are administrative impartiality and judicial constraints associated with stronger corruption control in GCC public administration between 2010 and 2024?

The article's argument is that corruption control in Gulf administration cannot be understood without attending to the legal conditions under which discretion is exercised. Where administration is more impartial and executive authority more effectively constrained, control of corruption should, in principle, be stronger. The mechanism is not that law abolishes corruption. It is that legal institutions structure discretion, raise the reviewability of administrative decisions, and narrow the space for personalised or politically mediated outcomes (La Porta et al., 1999; Djankov et al., 2003; La Porta et al., 2004). The claim concerns the ordinary legality of administration, not the sufficiency of anti-corruption law.

That distinction matters in the Gulf. GCC states have invested heavily in administrative modernisation, regulatory reform, digital government and diversification, typically framed in the language of competitiveness and investment attractiveness. But the managerial architecture of the state cannot be cleanly separated from its legal architecture. Modernisation may sharpen operational performance; its integrity effects depend on whether public bodies apply rules impartially, whether discretion is legally reviewable, and whether regulatory decisions follow predictable procedure rather than informal influence (Rauch and Evans, 2000; Dahlström et al., 2012; Cingolani et al., 2015). The legal structure of administration is, on this view, a precondition of administrative performance rather than a postscript to it.

The conceptual foundation is the quality-of-government literature. Rothstein and Teorell (2008) treat impartiality as the defining property of good institutions: public authority exercised without favouritism, arbitrariness or discrimination. For corruption research this shifts the question from whether anti-corruption institutions formally exist to how they actually operate. An administration may possess elaborate integrity procedures and still generate corruption risk if those procedures are applied selectively. A more impartial administration works as a routine integrity mechanism, narrowing the space available for favouritism and discretionary abuse (Dahlström et al., 2012; Persson et al., 2013).

Courts supply the second strand. Judicial constraints are used here functionally—institutional limits capable of checking arbitrary executive action—rather than as a label for any particular constitutional model. The distinction is essential in a region where legal systems differ in history, court structure, administrative review, and the practical relationship between judicial bodies and the executive. Where judicial constraints are stronger, administrative decisions are more likely to be reasoned, legally defensible and open to challenge (Feld and Voigt, 2003; Haggard et al., 2008; Skaaning, 2010).

The comparative literature has worked through the causes and consequences of corruption across development, competition, political institutions, resource wealth and accountability (Ades and Di Tella, 1999; Montinola and Jackman, 2002; Lederman et al., 2005; Serra, 2006; Pellegrini and Gerlagh, 2008), and GCC and MENA studies have examined oil rents, governance, investment and corruption outcomes (Ross, 2001; Smith, 2004; Saha and Ben Ali, 2017; Hakimi and Hamdi, 2017; Belloumi and Alshehry, 2021; Al-Faryan and Shil, 2022). What is largely missing is attention to the specifically legal-administrative channels through which corruption may be constrained in Gulf administration. The gap is conceptual and methodological as much as geographical: administrative impartiality, judicial constraint and the reviewability of executive action have rarely been operationalised as measurable variables in comparative corruption studies of the region.

To address it, the article uses a balanced country-year panel of the six GCC states over 2010–2024. Control of corruption is measured through WGI (Kaufmann et al., 2010); the legal-institutional regressors—rigorous and impartial public administration, and judicial constraints on executive power—are drawn from V-Dem; regulatory quality and political stability are controls. Given six cross-sectional units, the empirical analysis is read with deliberate restraint and makes no claim to causal identification. It examines legal-institutional associations that bear on debates over administrative reform, judicial constraint and public-sector integrity in the Gulf.

The contribution is threefold, and the article is candid that part of it lies in a well-diagnosed null. First, it recasts corruption control in the GCC as a legal-institutional governance problem rather than a purely economic, political or enforcement one. Second, it introduces administrative impartiality and judicial constraint as measurable legal variables in the comparative study of Gulf administration, and shows that their direct association with corruption-control perceptions is neither stable nor straightforward—while regulatory quality is. Third, by tracing that pattern to identifiable sources, it shows how legal institutions function as administrative and regulatory conditions for integrity rather than as formal guarantees or after-the-fact enforcement, and what aggregate indicators can and cannot register about them. The article sits at the intersection of public law, comparative governance and public-sector management.

## 2. Literature review and hypotheses

### 2.1 Corruption control and governance in resource-rich and GCC contexts

Comparative scholarship has long treated corruption control as a function of institutional quality, political accountability, economic structure and administrative capacity. Early work established that corruption depresses growth, distorts investment and degrades public decision-making (Mauro, 1995; Shleifer and Vishny, 1993). Later studies refined the account through competition, decentralisation, political institutions, accountability and the credibility of administration (Ades and Di Tella, 1999; Fisman and Gatti, 2002; Lederman et al., 2005; Treisman, 2007). The recurring implication is that corruption reflects an institutional environment, not merely individual conduct.

That implication sharpens in resource-rich states. Oil wealth can strengthen state capacity by supplying fiscal resources while simultaneously weakening pressure for accountability and raising the value of controlling discretionary allocation (Ross, 2001; Smith, 2004). The interaction between resource wealth, corruption and institutional incentives is neither uniform nor mechanical (Bhattacharyya and Hodler, 2010; Arezki and Brueckner, 2011). The operative question is not whether resource wealth produces corruption but whether public institutions can convert fiscal capacity into impartial, accountable, legally constrained administration.

The GCC occupies a distinctive position in that debate: resource-rich states with large public sectors, active reform agendas and growing concern for regulatory credibility and diversification. Regional studies have examined corruption, investment, growth and governance in GCC and MENA settings (Hakimi and Hamdi, 2017; Saha and Ben Ali, 2017; Belloumi and Alshehry, 2021; Al-Faryan and Shil, 2022). That work is valuable for context, but it tends to operate at the level of macroeconomic performance, oil rents and aggregate governance quality, rather than the legal-administrative mechanisms through which corruption is constrained or enabled in the exercise of public authority.

This article takes a narrower route. Whether governance matters in general is settled. The question here is whether two specific institutional features—rigorous and impartial administration, and judicial constraint on executive power—are associated with stronger corruption control. The distinction is analytically load-bearing: corruption control depends not only on policy declarations or enforcement intensity but on the routine conditions under which officials decide, apply rules and exercise discretion. Regulatory quality and political stability enter as controls because they capture relevant background conditions (Kaufmann et al., 2010); they are not the study's legal-institutional hypotheses.

### 2.2 Administrative impartiality as a legal-institutional constraint

Administrative impartiality bears on corruption because it concerns how public authority is applied, not merely the terms on which it is conferred. Rothstein and Teorell (2008) define impartial institutions as those that implement laws and policies without regard to anything about the citizen or case not specified in the relevant rule. That definition redirects analysis from institutional design to institutional conduct: not whether rules exist, but whether they are applied without favouritism or informal preference.

The link to corruption control is immediate. Corruption typically personalises public decisions—access mediated by connection, enforcement calibrated to political proximity, discretion bent to particular interests. An impartial administration narrows those openings by making the application of rules more predictable. It does not abolish discretion, which modern administration cannot function without; it disciplines discretion by embedding it in routines, standards and professional expectations that constrain unequal treatment and informal bargaining (Rothstein and Teorell, 2008; Persson et al., 2013).

The bureaucratic-quality literature supports this. Merit recruitment and professionalisation are associated with stronger bureaucratic performance (Rauch and Evans, 2000); meritocratic bureaucracies may deter corruption by insulating careers from clientelist pressure (Dahlström et al., 2012); state capacity and bureaucratic autonomy condition development outcomes (Cingolani et al., 2015). These studies differ in method but converge on one point: the internal organisation of administration shapes public integrity.

In legal terms, impartiality constrains because it narrows the grounds on which public decisions may legitimately rest. Officials must decide on legally relevant criteria rather than personal preference, political proximity or private exchange. This dual character—administrative value and legal value—gives impartiality particular weight in a region where large public sectors, regulatory transformation and diversification all strain the predictability of public action.

> **H1.** Rigorous and impartial public administration is positively associated with control of corruption in GCC countries.

### 2.3 Judicial constraints, executive power and corruption control

Judicial constraints demand careful definition in comparative work, and especially in the GCC. The concept is used functionally—institutional limits capable of checking arbitrary executive action—not as a descriptor of a particular judicial model. GCC systems differ in history, court structure, administrative review and the practical relationship between courts and the executive; a functional definition lets the analysis proceed without assuming that judicial review operates identically, or with identical effect, across them.

The institutional literature suggests courts shape governance by limiting opportunism, raising legal predictability and increasing the expected cost of arbitrary action (La Porta et al., 1998, 1999; Feld and Voigt, 2003; Haggard et al., 2008). The effect runs not only through adjudication but through anticipation: where review is credible, administrative actors have stronger reason to reason carefully, follow procedure and document a legal justification. That matters for corruption control because corrupt or arbitrary decisions depend on opacity, weak reviewability and limited consequences.

The rule-of-law dimension reinforces the point and complicates it. Skaaning (2010) notes that the rule of law resists precise measurement because its components—legality, limits on power, judicial oversight—do not always move together. Judicial constraint is treated here as one component of the legal environment in which administrative power is exercised, not as a proxy for the rule of law entire. Stronger judicial constraints may support corruption control by making executive decisions more contestable and narrowing the space for officials acting without legal accountability.

There is also a conditional logic. Administrative impartiality disciplines conduct from inside the bureaucracy; judicial constraint applies external pressure by subjecting selective action to review. Where both hold, the integrity effect may exceed either alone. An impartial administration insulated from review remains exposed to political override; judicial oversight without impartial administrative practice tends to be reactive rather than preventive. Together they constitute a more legally structured environment for corruption control.

> **H2.** Judicial constraints on executive power are positively associated with control of corruption in GCC countries.
>
> **H3.** Judicial constraints on executive power strengthen the positive association between rigorous and impartial public administration and control of corruption in GCC countries.

## 3. Data and methodology

The study uses a balanced country-year panel of the six GCC states—Bahrain, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates—for 2010–2024. The unit of analysis is the country-year, giving 90 observations. The sample is confined to the GCC, rather than a wider heterogeneous region, because the research question concerns legal-institutional conditions in Gulf administration specifically; this improves comparability across broadly similar systems while preserving the variation needed for panel analysis. The period spans a phase of substantial administrative reform, diversification and regulatory modernisation across the region.

The dependent variable is control of corruption, from the WGI. The estimate reflects perceptions of the extent to which public power is exercised for private gain, capturing petty and grand corruption and state capture (Kaufmann et al., 2010). It is standard in comparative governance research and fits a study concerned with corruption control as a broad institutional outcome rather than with individual prosecutions or sector-specific offences.

The two explanatory variables come from V-Dem. *Rigorous and impartial public administration* captures the extent to which officials implement laws and policies rigorously and impartially; it aligns directly with the argument that impartiality operates as a legal-institutional constraint on personalised decision-making. *Judicial constraints on executive power* captures the extent to which judicial institutions can constrain executive action, including judicial independence and compliance with decisions; it is the principal judicial measure because the concern is institutional limits on executive discretion rather than courts as isolated actors.

Two controls are included. Regulatory quality (WGI) captures the state's capacity to formulate and implement sound policies and regulations. Political stability and absence of violence/terrorism (WGI) captures the broader political environment in which administrative and judicial institutions operate (Kaufmann et al., 2010). Both reduce omitted-variable bias and help separate the legal-institutional mechanisms from general governance conditions; neither is a core hypothesis.

**Table I. Variable description and sources**

| Variable role | Variable | Operational measure | Source | Expected relationship |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dependent variable | Control of corruption | WGI control of corruption estimate | WGI | — |
| Main independent variable | Rigorous and impartial public administration | V-Dem rigorous and impartial public administration indicator | V-Dem | Positive |
| Judicial independent variable | Judicial constraints on executive power | V-Dem judicial constraints on executive power indicator | V-Dem | Positive |
| Control variable | Regulatory quality | WGI regulatory quality estimate | WGI | Control |
| Control variable | Political stability | WGI political stability and absence of violence/terrorism estimate | WGI | Control |
| Robustness dependent variable | Rule of law | WGI rule of law estimate | WGI | Alternative outcome |
| Robustness judicial variable | High court independence | V-Dem high court independence indicator | V-Dem | Alternative judicial measure |
| Additional institutional control | Property rights | Heritage Foundation property rights score | Heritage Foundation Index of Economic Freedom | Additional control |

The baseline model estimates the association between the two legal-institutional variables and control of corruption, conditioning on the controls and on country and year fixed effects:

> Control of corruption*ᵢₜ* = β₀ + β₁ Administrative impartiality*ᵢₜ* + β₂ Judicial constraints*ᵢₜ* + β₃ Regulatory quality*ᵢₜ* + β₄ Political stability*ᵢₜ* + μ*ᵢ* + λ*ₜ* + ε*ᵢₜ*

where *i* indexes country and *t* year. Country fixed effects absorb time-invariant characteristics—legal tradition, administrative history, political structure. Year fixed effects absorb common shocks and regional or global trends. The specification thus exploits within-country variation over time while controlling for shared temporal effects.

The choice between fixed effects (FE) and random effects (RE) is not treated as automatic. With only six cross-sectional units, two-way FE absorbs many parameters relative to the available degrees of freedom, and conventional cluster-robust inference is unreliable with so few clusters. A Hausman test does not reject the null that the country-specific effects are uncorrelated with the regressors (χ² = 2.76, df = 4, p = 0.598), so the RE estimator is consistent and more efficient here. The analysis therefore treats RE as the preferred estimator while reporting the pooled and FE models alongside it, so that within-country and cross-sectional information are both visible rather than discarded. Serial correlation is assessed with a Wooldridge test, and inference in the two-way FE models is additionally checked with Driscoll–Kraay standard errors, robust to cross-sectional dependence and heteroskedasticity in short panels.

The conditional hypothesis is tested through an interaction model:

> Control of corruption*ᵢₜ* = β₀ + β₁ Administrative impartiality*ᵢₜ* + β₂ Judicial constraints*ᵢₜ* + β₃ (Administrative impartiality*ᵢₜ* × Judicial constraints*ᵢₜ*) + β₄ Regulatory quality*ᵢₜ* + β₅ Political stability*ᵢₜ* + μ*ᵢ* + λ*ₜ* + ε*ᵢₜ*

A positive coefficient on the interaction would be consistent with impartiality and judicial constraint functioning as complementary mechanisms. The reading is necessarily cautious: the model identifies whether the association between impartial administration and corruption control varies with the level of judicial constraint, not whether judicial constraint produces corruption control through a mechanical causal pathway.

The analysis proceeds in five stages: descriptive statistics; country- and year-level comparisons; correlation and variance-inflation (VIF) diagnostics; the regression sequence (pooled OLS, country FE, year FE, two-way FE, and the RE model selected by Hausman, with Wooldridge and Driscoll–Kraay diagnostics); and robustness checks using WGI rule of law as an alternative outcome, V-Dem high court independence as an alternative judicial measure, and Heritage Foundation property rights as an additional control.

The design has acknowledged limits. The GCC sample is small by construction, improving comparability but constraining power; results read as institutional association, not causal proof. Governance indicators—perception-based WGI or expert-coded V-Dem—cannot capture every doctrinal or procedural feature of a domestic legal system. The study uses publicly available country-level data, involves no human participants, and required no ethical approval. It follows transparency principles by identifying all sources, reporting the modelling strategy, and keeping legal interpretation, empirical association and causal inference distinct.

The legal-institutional variables do not behave as hypothesised. Administrative impartiality is positive but insignificant in the pooled model, turns negative and significant once country fixed effects enter (Model 2), and is negative and significant only at the ten per cent level in the two-way specification (Model 4). Rather than a stable positive association, within-country movements in the impartiality measure are not consistently aligned with short-term improvements in perceived corruption control. **H1 is not supported.**

Judicial constraints are equally unstable. The coefficient is negative and significant in the pooled and year-FE models, then positive but insignificant once country fixed effects are introduced; in the two-way model it is positive and very imprecise. The negative pooled association most likely reflects cross-country differences rather than a within-country relationship. **H2 is not supported.**

Model 5 tests H3. The interaction is positive—directionally consistent with the hypothesis—but insignificant. The data do not provide evidence of a conditional effect in this sample, though the sign remains compatible with the theoretical expectation. **H3 is not confirmed.**

The one robust pattern is regulatory quality. Its coefficient is positive in all five models and significant in four, including the two-way fixed-effects and interaction specifications. Within the GCC, corruption-control perceptions track regulatory capacity more consistently than short-term changes in administrative impartiality or judicial constraint. Political stability is positive throughout but loses significance once both country and year effects are absorbed.

The high R-squared values warrant a specific caution. They reflect the small panel and the country and year dummies, not strong explanatory power for the legal-institutional variables, and overall fit must be distinguished from within fit. The two-way model's overall R-squared (0.943) is driven largely by the dummies; the within R-squared—the share of within-country, within-year variation explained by the covariates—is 0.432. The legal-institutional variables thus contribute moderately, not overwhelmingly, and the apparently high fit does not signal predictive strength for impartiality or judicial constraint.

These results do not invalidate the legal-institutional framework; they require a restrained reading. Impartiality and judicial constraint may be institutionally relevant while their measurable association with corruption control remains indirect—an outcome traceable to limited within-country variation, the gap between expert-coded and perception-based indicators, or the gradual operation of legal reform. The appropriate response is careful interpretation, not abandonment of the argument.

Because the Hausman test did not reject the random-effects specification (χ² = 2.76, p = 0.598), RE is the preferred estimator, and its results are read as the primary evidence; the FE models are retained to expose the within-country structure. Under RE, regulatory quality (β = 0.458, p < 0.01) and political stability (β = 0.295, p < 0.01) are positively and significantly associated with corruption control. Judicial constraints carry a positive but insignificant coefficient (β = 0.681, p = 0.144). Administrative impartiality is significant but negative (β = −0.354, p < 0.01). The RE estimates reinforce the central pattern: regulatory quality is the most consistent legal-institutional correlate of corruption control, while the direct association of administrative impartiality is neither stable in sign nor consistent with H1. Two diagnostics support the inference. A Wooldridge test gives no evidence of first-order serial correlation (p = 0.762), and Driscoll–Kraay standard errors—robust to cross-sectional dependence—leave regulatory quality significant at one per cent in the two-way FE model (p = 0.004), with administrative impartiality only marginal (p = 0.051) and judicial constraints insignificant (p = 0.303). The substantive conclusion is robust to both the estimator and the form of the standard errors.

## 5. Discussion

The results resist a simple reading. They do not support a direct, consistent claim that administrative impartiality and judicial constraints predict corruption control in the GCC. Nor do they license the opposite conclusion that legal-institutional mechanisms are irrelevant. The defensible interpretation is qualified: legal institutions matter conceptually, but their measurable relationship with corruption control is conditioned by regulatory capacity, the slow temporality of institutional change, limited within-country variation, and the structural difference between expert-coded institutional variables and perception-based outcomes.

The strongest empirical pattern is the positive, comparatively stable association between regulatory quality and corruption control—and it is more than a control-variable artefact. Regulatory quality is not external to legal governance. It reflects the state's capacity to formulate and implement credible rules, structure market conduct and administer authority predictably (Kaufmann et al., 2010). In the GCC, corruption-control perceptions respond more visibly to the quality of regulatory systems than to narrower indicators of impartiality or judicial constraint. This does not reduce corruption control to managerial efficiency; it suggests that the legal architecture of integrity operates most measurably through the broader regulatory environment in which administrative decisions are made.

That reading refines the argument rather than discarding it. The original expectation was a positive association for impartiality and judicial constraint; the results show the relationship is not linear within this short panel. Impartiality is theoretically central—corruption depends on selective treatment, favouritism and personalised discretion (Rothstein and Teorell, 2008; Persson et al., 2013)—yet within-country changes in the V-Dem impartiality measure are not consistently aligned with short-term movements in perceived corruption control. One explanation is that impartiality functions as a background condition rather than an immediately visible driver, disciplining routine administration gradually through professional standards and internal procedure in ways that do not quickly move aggregate external perception.

A second explanation concerns the temporality of legal reform. Legal change ordinarily takes formal shape before it produces measurable administrative effects. New procedures, review mechanisms, compliance routines and merit-based appointment practices may need years to alter public expectations or external assessments, particularly where reform is executive-led, sector-specific or unevenly implemented. The absence of an immediate positive association is not evidence that reform is ineffective; it is consistent with effects that are delayed, mediated, or insufficiently captured by contemporaneous country-year indicators.

The judicial-constraints results point the same way. The pooled and year-FE models show a negative association; the country-FE models produce positive but imprecise coefficients. Cross-country differences appear to drive the simple association, and once stable country characteristics are controlled, the relationship becomes uncertain. From a comparative public-law standpoint this is intelligible. Judicial constraints in GCC systems operate within different histories, court structures and review arrangements; a single country-level index may capture meaningful variation while remaining unable to describe doctrinal differences in standing, jurisdiction, remedies, administrative procedure, enforcement of judgments, or the practical authority of courts over executive action.

The interaction is instructive too. Its coefficient is positive and directionally consistent with H3 but insignificant. The honest conclusion is cautious: the data provide no robust evidence of a conditional effect in this sample, yet the sign remains compatible with the proposition that legal-institutional constraints operate jointly rather than independently—worth preserving in the theory even where the empirical result is inconclusive.

Measurement deserves its own emphasis, because it is where method and substance meet. The article combines perception-based WGI estimates with expert-coded V-Dem measures. The combination is common and defensible, but it has interpretive consequences. Perceptions of corruption control may respond to visible enforcement campaigns, regulatory reform or general confidence in institutions—factors that move differently from expert assessments of impartiality or judicial independence. The mixed findings may therefore reflect not only institutional complexity but the difficulty of translating legal concepts into directly comparable quantitative indicators.

The panel's size and structure compound this. The sample deliberately includes only six countries, improving comparability but constraining power, and much of the relevant institutional difference is cross-sectional rather than within-country over time. The descriptive evidence shows GCC states differing meaningfully in corruption control, impartiality, judicial constraint and regulatory quality—but fixed-effects models rely on within-country variation, and where legal-institutional variables are stable over time (as Section 4.2 shows judicial constraints to be), their effects become imprecise once country effects are absorbed.

Two implications follow for legal scholarship. First, legal-institutional variables should not be treated as simple predictors whose effects appear immediately in aggregate outcomes. Public law operates through indirect pathways: impartiality structures the reasons and standards of decision-making; judicial constraint shapes reviewability and the executive's anticipation of challenge. These mechanisms may matter deeply even when their contemporaneous correlation with perception-based indicators is weak. Second, the findings argue for more doctrinally informed measurement. Future work on corruption control in GCC administration should not rest on broad governance indicators alone; it should examine administrative procedure, judicial-review practice, enforcement of court decisions, procurement rules, conflict-of-interest regimes and the institutional design of anti-corruption bodies.

In practical terms, the legal effectiveness of anti-corruption reform in GCC systems may depend less on the formal existence of integrity institutions than on the procedural quality of administrative action—reason-giving, reviewability, documentary discipline, procurement transparency and enforceable limits on discretion. These are legal conditions, not merely technical devices: they make public power more visible, contestable and disciplined, joining the doctrinal concerns of public law to the managerial concerns of institutional performance and accountability. If regulatory quality is the most consistent correlate of corruption control here, reform should extend beyond punitive enforcement to the regulatory environment in which decisions are made—clearer rules, predictable procedures, transparent licensing and procurement, reasoned decisions and institutionalised review. A technically efficient regulatory system can still permit unequal treatment if it lacks impartial administration and meaningful review; judicial oversight is weaker where decisions are poorly recorded or reasoned. Regulatory quality, administrative impartiality and judicial constraint are best understood as mutually reinforcing conditions of integrity, not independent levers to be pulled separately.

The discussion therefore reframes rather than withdraws the central argument. The results do not establish that legal institutions have no role in corruption control; they show that, in GCC administration, the measurable relationship is complex, mediated and rarely immediate. That is a more modest conclusion than the hypotheses anticipated—and, for that reason, a more credible one. Corruption control in reforming Gulf administrations is not produced by any single mechanism. It emerges from the interaction of regulatory capacity, impartial implementation, reviewable executive action, and the gradual consolidation of legal-administrative routine.

## 6. Conclusion

This article asked whether rigorous and impartial public administration and judicial constraints on executive power are associated with stronger control of corruption in GCC public administration during 2010–2024, and treated the question as legal-institutional rather than economic or enforcement-based. Corruption control was understood as an outcome shaped by the ordinary legality of administration—how discretion is structured, decisions reviewed, and public authority made predictable, impartial and accountable.

The empirical findings are deliberately modest. None of the three hypotheses is confirmed in any straightforward sense: administrative impartiality shows no stable positive association across the main models; judicial constraints change sign once country fixed effects enter; the interaction is positive in direction but insignificant. Regulatory quality is the one consistent correlate of corruption control, surviving every specification, both estimators, and the alternative-outcome and alternative-measure checks. In the GCC, corruption-control perceptions are more visibly aligned with the quality of regulatory systems than with short-term movements in narrower legal-institutional indicators.

These results refine the argument rather than reject it. Legal-institutional mechanisms remain theoretically important, but their measurable relationship with corruption control is neither immediate nor linear. Impartiality and judicial constraint may operate through slower, less visible pathways—improving the quality of public decisions, reducing discretionary opacity, strengthening reviewability—even when those effects do not register in annual country-level indicators. The analysis therefore resists the inference that legal reform translates automatically into short-term gains in aggregate corruption-control scores.

The theoretical contribution is to reframe corruption control in GCC administration as a legal-institutional governance problem, bringing impartiality and judicial constraint into the analysis as distinct mechanisms situated within a wider regulatory ecosystem rather than as isolated predictors. The methodological contribution is more specific and, given the results, more pointed: operationalising legal-institutional concepts in a balanced GCC panel offers a structured way to test legal claims comparatively while exposing the limits of doing so. WGI and V-Dem indicators are valuable but capture different layers of institutional reality, and perception-based and expert-coded measures need not move together. Future research should pair panel analysis with doctrinally sensitive indicators—administrative procedure, judicial review, procurement transparency, conflict-of-interest regulation and enforcement of judgments.

For reform, the implication is concrete. Anti-corruption strategy in GCC administration should not rest on punitive enforcement or the formal creation of integrity institutions alone. The strongest pattern in this study points to regulatory quality, which argues for strengthening the procedural environment of public decision-making: clearer rules, transparent procurement, reasoned decisions, reliable documentation, accessible review and enforceable limits on discretion. Such measures connect legal accountability to administrative performance and make corruption control credible in practice.

The study's limits are deliberate. Six countries over fifteen years improves regional comparability but constrains power, and legal-institutional effects may need longer horizons than the panel allows. Country-level indicators cannot capture domestic doctrinal variation or sector-specific reform. Future work should examine individual GCC jurisdictions in depth, incorporate lagged institutional effects, draw on sectoral data, and build legal indicators grounded directly in administrative law and judicial practice. Corruption control in reforming Gulf administrations cannot be explained by any single mechanism. Regulatory capacity is the most visible empirical channel, but it operates within a broader legal architecture in which administrative impartiality, judicial reviewability and procedural discipline remain central. The task for research and reform alike is to understand how these legal conditions become embedded in the everyday conduct of administration—not as reform rhetoric, but as enforceable routines that, over time, translate into more credible control of public power.
